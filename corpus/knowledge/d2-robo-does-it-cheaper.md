# "Robo-advisors do it cheaper"

> A cost-and-structure comparison; concedes the fee point where it's true and corrects it where it isn't. Robo fees flagged variable; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"StashAway and Versa do the same thing for a fraction of the cost — globally diversified portfolios, low fees, no 5% sales charge. Why pay a unit trust's loads when a robo does it cheaper?"

### The true part

Concede the fee point squarely where it holds: **StashAway Invest charges 0.8%/yr** on the first RM150,000 (tiering down to 0.2% above RM3m) and **no sales charge** — genuinely cheaper than a Public Mutual equity fund's **1.5%/yr** management fee plus a one-off entry load (corpus/data/datasets/robo-fee-tiers.json). On cost alone, at the lower end the robo wins, and the absence of an entry charge is a real advantage. A cost-focused saver is right that this is a cheaper way to hold a diversified portfolio.

### The variable it ignores

Two ignored variables: **"robo" is not one price, and the cheaper wrapper is a different wrapper**. First, the cheapness isn't uniform — **Versa Invest's core portfolios charge 1.5%/yr** (the AHAM fund-level fee; Versa itself adds no platform fee) plus 0.04% trustee, which is *about the same* annual cost as a unit trust (corpus/data/charts/robo-vs-ut-cost-feature.yaml). So "robos do it cheaper" is true for StashAway and roughly false for Versa Invest; the slogan flattens a real spread. Second, and more important, a robo *Invest* account is a **taxable wrapper** — it does **not** carry the RM3,000 standalone **PRS tax relief** a PRS-registered fund earns (a separate line from the EPF+life cap, worth RM390 at the 13% bracket up to RM720 at 24%, sunset YA2030; corpus/knowledge/b-relief-stacking.md) — and robo Invest money can't be funded from your EPF Akaun Persaraan savings the way a unit trust can via EPF i-Invest. All three (StashAway, Versa, Public Mutual) offer a Shariah option, so that isn't the difference. The honest turn is not "the unit trust is cheaper" — at the low end it isn't. It's that the comparison is cost-and-structure: the relief wrapper and EPF access are worth real money the fee table never shows, and whether they outweigh StashAway's fee saving depends on the saver's tax bracket and goals.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver in a low or zero tax bracket who doesn't value the PRS relief or EPF-MIS access and wants the lowest-cost diversified portfolio, the myth is right — StashAway's fee advantage is genuine and may well be the better deal. For the saver in a higher bracket optimising retirement, it changes the answer: the RM3,000 relief alone can exceed the annual fee difference, and a robo Invest account doesn't carry it — so "cheaper" on the fee line isn't the same as cheaper after tax.
