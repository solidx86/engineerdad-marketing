# Shariah vs Conventional PMB Funds — Structural Differences

> Figures reflect publicly published PMB factsheets as of 2026-05-31; fund-specific values change quarterly — verify against the current factsheet. Not personalised advice.

## English

### The framing: Shariah is a filter, not a separate asset class

A Shariah-compliant PMB fund is not a different category of investment — it is a conventional equity, balanced, fixed-income, or money-market fund with a permissibility filter applied to its investable universe. The risk-return profile depends primarily on the underlying asset class; the Shariah screening layer introduces structural differences in portfolio composition, purification mechanics, and tracking error relative to the conventional sibling.

---

### How Shariah screening works

PMB Shariah funds comply with the SC Shariah Advisory Council (SAC) guidelines, which apply a two-layer screen:

**Layer 1 — Business-activity screen**
Excludes companies whose core business involves any of the following:
- Riba (interest-based financial services in the conventional sense, e.g., conventional banking, conventional insurance)
- Alcohol production or distribution
- Tobacco
- Pork-related products
- Gambling and gaming
- Weapons and defence (discretionary — the SC carves out some categories)
- Entertainment activities deemed non-permissible under SAC guidance

**Layer 2 — Financial-ratio screen**
Even companies that pass the business-activity screen may fail the financial-ratio screen if their financial structure carries too much interest-bearing debt or too much cash held in interest-bearing instruments. The SC publishes benchmark ratios (e.g., interest-bearing debt / total assets below a specified threshold). Companies near or at the threshold are re-assessed at each semi-annual SC Shariah securities review.

The SC publishes its Shariah-compliant securities list twice yearly (April and October). Funds adjust their holdings after each review.

---

### Purification

When a Shariah fund holds a stock that subsequently fails the SC screening (or when a holding generates income from non-permissible activities), the fund must **purify** the non-permissible portion. Purification means the tainted income is identified, quantified, and donated to charity — it is removed from the fund's distributable income.

- **Operational implication:** Purification is an administrative process managed at the fund level; individual investors do not receive a separate purification invoice.
- **Performance implication:** In theory, purified amounts reduce distributable income slightly, but the amounts are typically a minor fraction of total fund income.
- **Transparency:** PMB Shariah fund reports disclose the purification amounts for the period.

### The fee and tracking-error differential

| Dimension | Shariah fund | Conventional sibling |
|---|---|---|
| Annual management fee | Comparable — typically within 0.10–0.25% p.a. of the conventional equivalent | Base case |
| Sales charge | Same rate structure — verify PHS | Base case |
| Tracking error vs conventional benchmark | Higher — Shariah screen excludes some index constituents | Lower (fund holds more of the full index) |
| Sector exposure | May be underweight financials (conventional banks excluded) and overweight sectors with high Shariah compliance (utilities, infrastructure, select tech) | Full-index exposure |

The tracking error differential is the structural cost of the Shariah filter. Because conventional banks and some large-cap financial institutions typically fail the financial-ratio screen, a Shariah equity fund will be underweight the financials sector compared to an FBM KLCI-weighted conventional fund. In years when Malaysian banks outperform, the Shariah fund will tend to lag; in years when banks underperform, the Shariah fund may lead.

---

### When the Shariah variant is structurally advantaged

- **In a financial-sector bear market:** The exclusion of conventional banks means a Shariah fund has less exposure to the sector most impacted by rising credit costs, tightening NIM, or banking-sector stress events.
- **For investors with a values-based mandate:** Shariah compliance is a non-negotiable constraint for many Muslim investors; the structural tracking error is the price of alignment and is accepted at the outset.
- **When Shariah-screened sectors outperform:** In cycles driven by technology, infrastructure, or consumer growth — sectors with high Shariah compliance rates — the fund's overweight in these areas can be a tailwind.

---

### When the Shariah variant is structurally disadvantaged

- **In a financial-sector bull market:** Strong bank and conventional financial-sector returns will benefit conventional equity funds more than Shariah funds, which carry little or no conventional bank exposure.
- **In a high-interest-rate environment where rate-sensitive financials rally:** Rising rates typically benefit bank net interest margins; Shariah funds miss this exposure.
- **For investors comparing fee-adjusted returns on a short window:** The same management fee structure, but potentially higher tracking error vs the benchmark, can make the Shariah fund look like an underperformer in years the excluded sectors do well.

---

### The "Shariah is safer" misconception

The Shariah screen excludes certain business activities and leveraged structures, which sometimes leads investors to assume Shariah funds are lower risk than their conventional equivalents. This is not correct:

- A Shariah equity fund is still an equity fund — it can lose 30–40% of NAV in a severe equity market downturn.
- The exclusion of high-leverage companies may produce slightly lower financial leverage on average across the portfolio, but this is a secondary effect, not a capital-protection mechanism.
- Shariah compliance is a permissibility filter, not a risk-management framework.

---

### Four things most investors get wrong about Shariah vs conventional

- **A Shariah fund and its conventional sibling are not interchangeable.** They will track differently in most market cycles because the Shariah-screened portfolio is materially different in sector composition, particularly with respect to financials and some industrial sub-sectors.
- **The Shariah fund's tracking error vs the FBM KLCI (a conventional index) is not a performance flaw — it is structural.** Comparing a Shariah equity fund to the FBM KLCI without accounting for the excluded-sector gap is a benchmark mismatch. Some PMB Shariah funds benchmark against the FTSE Bursa Malaysia Hijrah Shariah Index, which is the appropriate comparator.
- **Purification amounts are typically small but non-zero.** Investors who assume a Shariah fund delivers 100% permissible income from day one should understand that residual impermissible income can arise from transitional holdings; the fund cleans it through donation rather than passing it to investors.
- **A non-Muslim investor can hold a Shariah fund.** There is no regulatory restriction on non-Muslim investors choosing Shariah-screened funds; some choose them for non-religious reasons (e.g., preference for the sector tilt away from conventional financials). The compliance is at the fund level; investor religion is irrelevant.

*Framing:* The Shariah vs conventional conversation is often framed as a religious compliance question. For an analytical parent, it is also a portfolio-construction question: what sector tilts am I accepting, what tracking error am I buying, and does the fund's benchmark reflect those tilts fairly?
