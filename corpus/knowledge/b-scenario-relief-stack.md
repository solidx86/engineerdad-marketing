# Scenario — Dual-Income Household Optimising Combined Reliefs

> Tax figures reflect publicly published LHDN/PPA schedules as of 2026-05-31; tax rules change annually — confirm the active year with LHDN and consult a tax adviser. Not personalised tax advice. This is a synthesised illustrative scenario — it is not a real case. All income figures, contribution amounts, and tax-saved calculations are labelled assumptions. The purpose of this scenario is to show the structural relief stacking logic, not to project any specific individual's tax liability. Always model the individual's actual position with a tax adviser.

## English

### The scenario

Imagine a household with two earners. Person A is a salaried software engineer; Person B works as an independent consultant. Both are in their mid-30s with two young children. They have been paying income tax for a decade but have never methodically audited their reliefs beyond filing EPF contribution figures.

**Declared assumptions:**

| Variable | Person A | Person B |
|---|---|---|
| Gross annual income | RM96,000 | RM72,000 |
| Approximate chargeable income (after standard deductions, before the reliefs modelled here) | RM72,000 | RM52,000 |
| Approximate marginal rate | 21% | 13% |
| EPF contribution status | Mandatory 11% through payroll (~RM10,560/year) | EPF i-Saraan voluntary |
| Life insurance premium | RM3,000/year | RM2,400/year |
| PRS contribution before optimisation | None | None |
| SSPN contribution before optimisation | None | None |

*These income figures are illustrative. Real household numbers will differ; the structural logic is transferable.*

### Reliefs before optimisation

| Relief line | Person A (RM) | Person B (RM) |
|---|---|---|
| EPF contribution | 4,000 (capped; actual contribution exceeds cap) | 2,400 (via i-Saraan, within matching range) |
| Life insurance (within EPF+life shared pool) | 3,000 | 2,400 (fills remaining pool capacity) |
| EPF+life combined claimed | 7,000 (at cap) | 4,800 |
| PRS | 0 | 0 |
| SSPN (children's education savings) | 0 | 0 |

Combined claimed reliefs before optimisation: RM7,000 + RM4,800 = RM11,800 across the household.

### Reliefs after adding PRS and SSPN

Imagine both contribute RM3,000 each to PRS in the same tax year. They also open an SSPN account for their eldest child and make a combined net deposit of RM8,000 (split RM5,000/RM3,000 across both filers to match taxable income).

| Relief line | Person A (RM) | Person B (RM) |
|---|---|---|
| EPF+life combined (unchanged) | 7,000 | 4,800 |
| PRS (new) | 3,000 | 3,000 |
| SSPN net deposit (new) | 5,000 | 3,000 |
| Total reliefs this year | 15,000 | 10,800 |

Combined household reliefs after optimisation: RM25,800.

### Illustrative tax saved

| Item | Person A | Person B |
|---|---|---|
| Additional relief claimed (PRS + SSPN) | RM8,000 | RM6,000 |
| Marginal rate applied | 21% | 13% |
| Illustrative additional tax saved | RM1,680 | RM780 |
| Combined additional household tax saved | **RM2,460** | |

*This is the illustrative tax saving on the incremental relief only, applied at the stated marginal rates. Actual tax saved depends on the individual's exact chargeable income, applicable reliefs in the filing year, and whether the marginal rate band threshold is crossed. Confirm with a tax adviser.*

### Four takeaways

- **The marginal-rate asymmetry matters even within one household.** Person A saves RM1,680 on the same RM8,000 of incremental relief that saves Person B only RM780. Where possible, allocating larger reliefs (especially SSPN net deposits, which are flexible) to the higher-rate earner maximises the household tax reduction.
- **PRS gives both earners a standalone RM3,000 relief regardless of whether their EPF+life pool is full or empty.** Person A's EPF+life pool is maxed at RM7,000; Person B's is partially filled. Neither status affects the PRS relief availability — the RM3,000 PRS line is independent.
- **SSPN is stackable across both earners' returns for the same child's account.** The LHDN rules allow each parent to claim their own SSPN net deposit relief on their separate returns — both parents contributing to the same SSPN account can each claim on their respective returns (subject to LHDN's current interpretation; confirm with a tax adviser). This is one of the most underused optimisations in a dual-income household.
- **The combined tax saving (illustrative RM2,460) funds approximately 82% of the PRS contributions themselves (RM6,000 total PRS outlay across both filers).** At these income levels, the relief almost pays for the PRS contributions in the first year alone — before any investment return is considered. This changes the mental model from "saving" to "self-funding via tax rebate."

### What this scenario does not show

It does not show investment returns, fund selection, or long-run compounding. It also does not model the education savings impact of SSPN beyond the current-year tax relief. The purpose is narrow: to demonstrate that the relief stacking logic is structural and predictable, and that a household that has never audited its relief claims is likely leaving material tax savings on the table each year.

*Framing:* The scenario is not unusual — it describes the median EngineerDad-archetype household. Most analytical parents have the income to benefit meaningfully from full stacking but have never modelled it in one place. The conversation starts with the return form, not the product.
