# Risk Metric Glossary — PMB Portfolio Context

> Figures reflect publicly published PMB factsheets as of 2026-05-31; fund-specific values change quarterly — verify against the current factsheet. Not personalised advice.

## English

### Why risk metrics belong in every portfolio conversation

PMB factsheets and statements present at least three quantitative risk metrics alongside performance figures. Without a working definition of each, these numbers are noise — or worse, they are misread as safety guarantees. The five metrics below cover everything that appears on a standard PMB factsheet and everything a parent needs to ask about before constructing a portfolio.

---

### 1. Standard Deviation (SD)

**Definition:** The annualised dispersion of a fund's periodic returns around its mean return. Calculated from monthly NAV returns, then annualised. Expressed as a percentage.

**How to read it on a PMB factsheet:** The factsheet shows 3-year annualised SD. A figure of, say, 14% means that over the measurement window, the fund's annual return was within roughly ±14 percentage points of the mean about 68% of the time (one standard deviation band).

**What a "good" value looks like:** There is no universal good value — SD is meaningful only in comparison to a peer group in the same category, or against the fund's benchmark index. A domestic equity fund with SD of 12–16% is typical; a money-market fund with SD above 1% would be unusual.

**The trap:** Comparing SD across asset classes is invalid. A bond fund naturally has lower SD than an equity fund because the underlying instruments are less volatile — a lower SD does not make one manager "better" than another across categories.

---

### 2. Sharpe Ratio

**Definition:** (Fund Return − Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation. Measures return earned per unit of total volatility. A higher ratio is better — more return for each unit of risk taken.

**How to read it on a PMB factsheet:** PMB typically uses a domestic short-term interest rate (e.g., overnight policy rate or equivalent) as the risk-free proxy. A Sharpe ratio above 1.0 is considered solid in most equity markets; below 0.5 over a 3-year window suggests the fund is not being compensated well for its volatility.

**What a "good" value looks like:** Context-dependent. In a high-volatility year (e.g., 2020), most equity funds' Sharpe ratios compressed; comparing the same fund across market regimes is more meaningful than a single-year snapshot.

**The trap:** Sharpe treats upside and downside volatility equally. A fund that delivered a single +40% month (high upside volatility) will show a lower Sharpe than a fund with smoother but lower returns — this may mislead a risk-averse investor who only cares about losses.

---

### 3. Beta

**Definition:** The fund's return sensitivity to its benchmark index. A beta of 1.0 means the fund moves in lockstep with the index. Beta > 1.0 means the fund amplifies index moves (higher sensitivity). Beta < 1.0 means lower sensitivity.

**How to read it on a PMB factsheet:** Beta appears on some but not all PMB factsheets; it is more commonly found in institutional or detailed fund reports. Where present, it is calculated over 36 monthly return pairs (fund vs benchmark).

**What a "good" value looks like:** Depends entirely on intent. A parent wanting market exposure should expect beta near 1.0 for an equity index-type mandate. A genuinely active defensive mandate might target beta of 0.7–0.8 — accepting lower upside to reduce drawdown.

**The trap:** Beta is only meaningful if the benchmark is appropriate. A global equity fund compared against FBM KLCI will show a near-zero beta because the two have very different return drivers — this is a benchmark-mismatch artefact, not evidence of low market sensitivity.

---

### 4. Maximum Drawdown

**Definition:** The largest peak-to-trough decline in NAV over a specified period, expressed as a percentage. If a fund's NAV peaked at RM1.00 and subsequently fell to RM0.72 before recovering, the maximum drawdown is −28%.

**How to read it on a PMB factsheet:** Not always shown on the standard two-page factsheet; appears in detailed fund reports and some fund comparison tools. When reviewing for a parent with a specific drawdown tolerance (e.g., "I cannot afford to lose more than 20% of my education fund"), this is the most operationally relevant metric.

**What a "good" value looks like:** For an equity fund, maximum drawdowns of 30–50% over a full cycle including a market crisis (e.g., 2008, 2020) are not unusual. A drawdown below 10% on an equity fund over a long period should prompt the question of whether the fund is truly invested in equities.

**The trap:** Maximum drawdown is historical — it describes the worst outcome that happened, not the worst outcome that can happen. A fund with a −20% maximum drawdown in the past may experience a −40% drawdown in a future crisis the historical window did not include.

---

### 5. Tracking Error

**Definition:** The annualised standard deviation of the difference between the fund's return and its benchmark return, period by period. Low tracking error means the fund closely mirrors the benchmark; high tracking error means the fund diverges significantly — whether in a good or bad direction.

**How to read it on a PMB factsheet:** Tracking error appears primarily on factsheets for funds with an explicit mandate to track or stay close to a benchmark. It is less commonly disclosed for purely active mandates.

**What a "good" value looks like:** For a fund marketed as "close to the index", tracking error below 2–3% is expected. For an actively managed fund with a mandate to add alpha, tracking error of 5–10% is typical — active bets require deviation from the index. A Shariah fund vs its conventional benchmark will carry structural tracking error from the exclusion of screened-out sectors.

**The trap:** A low tracking error can mean the fund is closely managed to the benchmark — or it can mean the manager is "closet indexing" (charging active management fees while essentially replicating the index). The combination of low tracking error + fees comparable to active management + no alpha is the "closet indexer" red flag.

---

### Four things most investors get wrong about risk metrics

- **Standard deviation is not a loss probability.** It measures historical volatility — the width of the return distribution. An SD of 14% does not mean "you will lose 14%"; it describes the typical dispersion of returns, which includes both gains and losses.
- **A high Sharpe ratio on a short window (< 3 years) is nearly meaningless.** Any fund that happened to perform well in a short calm period will show a high Sharpe; this has very little predictive value for forward risk-adjusted returns.
- **Comparing risk metrics across asset classes is analytically invalid.** A bond fund's Sharpe of 1.5 cannot be compared to an equity fund's Sharpe of 0.8 as if the bond fund is "twice as good" — the denominators (standard deviations) are not comparable because the underlying risk types are fundamentally different.
- **Maximum drawdown tells you what happened, not what is possible.** A fund that has never experienced a drawdown worse than −15% in its 10-year history may still be capable of a −40% drawdown in a tail scenario the history did not include — this is especially true for funds that launched post-2009 and have only operated in a broadly rising equity market.

*Framing:* A parent who can read five numbers on a factsheet — SD, Sharpe, beta, max drawdown, and tracking error — and knows what comparison to make with each, can hold a peer-level technical conversation with any consultant. The goal is not to impress; it is to avoid being sold on one number (trailing return) while blind to the others.
