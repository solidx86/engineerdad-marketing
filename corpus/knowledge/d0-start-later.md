# "I'll start later, when I'm settled"

> Describes the mechanics and trade-offs of delaying contributions; all RM figures are illustrative model output at a stated assumption, dated, and not a forecast or guaranteed outcome. Nothing here is personalised advice — read the relevant Master Prospectus and Product Highlights Sheet (PHS) before investing.

## English

### The myth

"Right now things are tight — the house, the kids, the loans. Once I'm more settled, earning more, with fewer commitments, I'll have room to invest properly. Starting later, but bigger, gets me to the same place."

### The true part

The intuition that contributions are easier later is often correct: income usually rises with seniority and some commitments (a car loan, a study loan) do roll off. Waiting until there is real slack is a more sustainable plan than over-committing today and quitting in month three. If "later" means a few months to clear a genuine cash-flow crunch, the cost is small and the discipline gain is real. The concession holds — what the myth misprices is how steeply the cost of waiting climbs once "later" stretches into years.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is **time itself**, and it does not trade off linearly against contribution size. The start-age penalty makes this concrete: the same RM200/month at an 8% assumption, valued at age 18, is roughly RM100,000 if started at birth, RM57,000 if started at age 5, RM28,000 at age 10, and RM9,000 at age 15 (corpus/data/charts/start-age-penalty.yaml). Every five years of delay roughly halves the result — because the years lost are the *earliest* years, the ones that compound the longest.

The same shape governs retirement. The cost of closing the *same* Adequate-tier EPF gap runs roughly **RM408/month at age 25 → RM1,028 at age 30 → RM4,489 at age 40 → more than the entire salary at age 50** (corpus/proof/epf-shortfall-cases.md). "Start later, bigger" sounds symmetrical, but the maths is asymmetric: the later you start, the more you must contribute, and past a point the required top-up exceeds what any realistic income can supply. Figures are illustrative model output, not personalised advice, and not a forecast.

### Who it changes the answer for

For someone in a short, genuine crunch — a few months to rebuild a buffer — "later" is fine and the penalty is negligible. For the person using "when I'm settled" as an open-ended default, where "later" has already slipped several years and keeps slipping, the answer flips hard: the cheapest contribution they will ever make is the one available today, and waiting for the perfect moment is itself the most expensive choice on the table.
