# "Fund managers underperform, why pay for active"

> Concedes the SPIVA evidence; treats the weighted-alpha screen as a backward-looking selection signal, never a forecast or a scheme-specific performance promise. Past performance does not indicate future results. Not personalised advice; no individual fund named or recommended.

## English

### The myth

"The research says most active managers lose to their benchmark. So paying for active management is paying for underperformance — why not just index?"

### The true part

The evidence is real, it's strong, and the honest answer leads with it. S&P's SPIVA U.S. Year-End 2025 scorecard found **79% of active large-cap U.S. funds underperformed the S&P 500** in 2025, and long-horizon underperformance rates **exceed 80% across every region** S&P measures (corpus/data/datasets/spiva-behaviour-gap.json). The base rate for active management beating its benchmark, net of fees, is genuinely poor — especially against the U.S. large-cap index, which is an unusually hard benchmark to beat. Anyone defaulting to "assume active underperforms" is starting from the statistically correct prior, and the brand does not dispute it.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is that **the base rate is not destiny, and the active-vs-passive outcome differs by market and by selection** — but this must be stated carefully, as a backward-looking signal, never a forecast. SPIVA measures the *average* active fund, heavily weighted to the U.S. large-cap arena. In the Malaysian universe the picture has looked different: on EngineerDad's published weighted-alpha screen, **73.2% of Public Mutual equity funds, and 77.6% of funds with a three-year record, posted positive alpha** against their benchmark as at April 2026 (corpus/proof/fund-universe-stats-snapshot.md). That is a *backward-looking research signal about a past period* — it is **not** a prediction, **not** a promise about any fund's future, and **past performance does not indicate future results**; no individual fund is named, ranked or recommended here (a fund-specific view is a one-to-one conversation with a licensed consultant). The honest turn is narrow and defensible: "most active underperforms" is a true average, but averages hide selection — alpha (return above benchmark; corpus/data/charts/alpha-vs-return.yaml) is the metric that separates managers who added value in the past from those who didn't, and a disciplined screen for it is a way to *select within* the active universe rather than a claim that active beats passive. And in a market with no cheap, deep local index-fund option, "just index" is less frictionless for a Malaysian-equity allocation than the slogan assumes.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver whose target exposure is U.S. large-cap, where a cheap index is available and SPIVA is most damning, the myth is right — indexing that slice is hard to beat and a reasonable default. For the saver dismissing *all* active management on the U.S. average alone, it changes the answer: the base rate varies by market, alpha-based selection is a legitimate (backward-looking, non-guaranteed) way to choose within the active universe, and "most underperform on average" is not the same as "this selection will" — a distinction that is evidence, not a promise.
