# Distribution Mechanics: Ex-date, NAV Drop, Reinvestment, and Why Distribution ≠ Profit

> Figures reflect publicly published PMB distribution policies as of 2026-05-31; consultant to confirm fund-specific distribution history and reinvestment terms against current PHS before use in campaign copy. Distributions are not guaranteed — a fund may choose not to declare a distribution in any period. Nothing here constitutes personalised advice.

## English

### What a distribution actually is

A PMB fund may periodically declare a **distribution** — a payout of income the fund has earned from its underlying assets (interest, dividends from equities, realised capital gains). The distribution is expressed in ringgit per unit (e.g. RM0.04 per unit). It is NOT equivalent to a stock dividend in a corporate equity sense, and PMB specifically uses the term "distribution" rather than "dividend" to avoid that confusion.

**The critical mechanic: distribution is NOT new wealth created at the moment of payout.**

### The ex-date NAV drop

On the **ex-distribution date** (ex-date), the fund's NAV is marked down by exactly the declared distribution amount:

| Date | Event | NAV example |
|---|---|---|
| Day before ex-date | Fund NAV includes accumulated income | RM1.00 |
| Ex-date | NAV drops by distribution amount | RM1.00 − RM0.04 = **RM0.96** |
| Payment / reinvestment date | Distribution paid out or reinvested | RM0.96 base + units purchased |

**Before the ex-date**: your RM10,000 holding (10,000 units × RM1.00 NAV) is worth RM10,000.
**On ex-date**: your 10,000 units × RM0.96 = RM9,600 in unit value + RM400 in declared distribution = RM10,000 total. Your portfolio value is unchanged at the moment of distribution.

### Auto-reinvestment

Most PMB accumulation/growth funds default to **automatic reinvestment** of distributions. When reinvestment is elected:

- The RM400 distribution is used to purchase additional units at the post-ex NAV (RM0.96 in our example).
- New units purchased: RM400 ÷ RM0.96 ≈ 416.67 additional units.
- Total units after reinvestment: 10,416.67 at RM0.96 = RM10,000.
- Net effect on day zero: portfolio value is exactly the same.

**Reinvestment does not add value on the day it executes.** What it does is preserve the compounding base — instead of receiving RM400 in cash (which sits idle), it buys units that then participate in future NAV growth.

### The pre-ex-date buying trap

Investors who buy a fund immediately before the ex-date and receive the distribution often perceive it as a "bonus" — but they are receiving a return of part of their own investment. The NAV they paid already included the about-to-be-distributed income; the distribution strips that income out and returns it. There is no free gain.

### Four things most investors don't know

- **On ex-date, the fund's NAV drops by exactly the declared distribution amount.** A distribution of RM0.04/unit on a RM1.00 NAV fund leaves a post-ex NAV of RM0.96. Total portfolio value is unchanged at that moment — the distribution is a transfer of value out of the NAV, not new wealth added to it.
- **Buying a fund just before the ex-date to "capture" the distribution is a tax-neutral at best, and logistically pointless, move for retail investors.** The investor pays a NAV that includes the about-to-be-stripped distribution, receives the distribution, and ends up with exactly what they started with — minus any sales charge paid to enter the fund.
- **Auto-reinvestment purchases new units at the post-ex NAV, not the pre-ex NAV.** Reinvested units are cheaper (post-ex price), so the same ringgit amount buys more units — but this is mechanical, not a bonus. The starting portfolio value is reconstructed, not increased.
- **PMB uses "distribution" not "dividend" deliberately.** The word "dividend" implies a share of corporate profit beyond what was originally invested; a unit trust distribution is a pass-through of income already inside the fund. Calling it a dividend misrepresents the economic event.
- **Distributions are not guaranteed.** A fund's distribution history does not constitute a promise of future distributions. In periods where the fund has not realised sufficient income or gains to support a payout, no distribution may be declared. An investor who builds a cash-flow plan around historical distribution levels is making an assumption the prospectus explicitly does not support.

### Reinvestment vs cash payout — the practical choice

For an investor in the accumulation phase (decades from retirement), auto-reinvestment is almost always the rational default: it keeps the entire portfolio working without manual redeployment. For an investor in the drawdown phase needing regular income, cash payout to a bank account makes practical sense — but the investor should understand that the underlying portfolio shrinks (in unit-value terms) each time a distribution is paid out in cash.

*Framing:* The distribution-equals-profit misconception is one of the most durable misunderstandings in unit trust marketing. Clearing it upfront is a credibility signal: the consultant who explains the NAV drop on ex-date is the one who is not trying to sell a yield illusion.
