# "Saving / FD is enough — investing is optional"

> Describes the mechanics and trade-offs of holding cash versus invested assets; the inflation figure used below is the DOSM headline-CPI average for the named window 2011–2025 (corpus/data/datasets/my-cpi-inflation.json), and all RM figures are illustrative of the mechanic, dated, and not a forecast. Nothing here is personalised advice — read the relevant Master Prospectus and Product Highlights Sheet (PHS) before investing.

## English

### The myth

"I keep my money in the bank and a fixed deposit. The balance only ever goes up, I can see it, and I won't lose any of it. Investing is for people chasing extra — for me, saving is enough."

### The true part

This is right about what cash and fixed deposits are *for*, and that part matters. Capital-stable instruments are the correct home for an emergency fund and for money you will spend soon — money you cannot afford to see fall in nominal terms. A fixed deposit's nominal balance does not drop, and that certainty has real value for short-horizon needs. Anyone without a cash buffer should build one before anything else. The concession is genuine: for short-term money, "saving is enough" is simply true.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is **inflation absorbing the interest over long horizons** — the gap between the number on the statement and what that number can actually buy. The myth watches the nominal balance rise and reads it as progress. But the relevant measure is real value.

Take RM100,000 in a 2% account over 18 years. The statement climbs to about RM143,000 — up 43% on paper. Yet over 2011–2025, Malaysian headline inflation averaged **2.0% a year** (DOSM annual CPI, corpus/data/datasets/my-cpi-inflation.json) — almost exactly what the account pays. Deflated at that average, the pile's purchasing power after 18 years is roughly **RM100,000 in today's money: zero real growth** (corpus/data/charts/inflation-vs-savings-real-value.yaml). The balance never fell on paper, but eighteen years of waiting bought nothing more than what the money could already buy on day one. Saving *preserved* the money; it did not *build* anything — so every ringgit of progress toward a long-horizon goal has to come from fresh contributions, none from growth.

The average also hides the year-to-year swings: in 2022 alone prices rose 3.4% while a 2% account fell behind; in 2020 prices fell 1.1% and cash gained. And the window matters — over the trailing 10 years (2016–2025) inflation averaged a lower 1.79%, at which a 2% account slightly *gains* real value (both windows in corpus/data/datasets/my-cpi-inflation.json). Headline CPI is also an economy-wide average: the specific costs a family is saving toward, such as education or medical care, follow their own price paths that this series does not measure. These are dated figures describing a mechanic, not a forecast, and not personalised advice.

### Who it changes the answer for

For short-horizon money — the emergency fund, next year's school fees, a near-term down payment — "saving is enough" is correct, and chasing return there would be the mistake. For **long-horizon money** — retirement decades away, a newborn's education fund — the answer flips: at the last 15 years' average inflation, a 2% account merely treads water, and in higher-inflation years it falls behind. Preservation is not the same job as growth, and a long-horizon goal needs the second job done. The honest framing is not cash *versus* investing but matching each instrument to its time horizon.
