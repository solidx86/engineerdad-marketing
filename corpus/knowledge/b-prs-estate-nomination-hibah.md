# PRS Estate, Nomination, and Hibah

> Tax figures reflect publicly published LHDN/PPA schedules as of 2026-05-31; tax rules change annually — confirm the active year with LHDN and consult a tax adviser. Not personalised tax advice. The nomination and estate rules summarised here are drawn from publicly published PPA and SC guidelines; the interaction with faraid and hibah for Muslim estates involves religious law that varies by state — members should consult a qualified estate planner or syariah practitioner alongside a financial consultant. This entry will carry additional BM coverage in a later pass given its particular relevance to the Muslim-parent register.

## English

### The nomination framework for PRS and unit trust

PRS and conventional unit trust funds both allow the member to nominate a beneficiary, but the legal mechanism differs:

| Vehicle | Nomination type | Effect on estate |
|---|---|---|
| PRS (through PPA) | Trust nomination | Proceeds paid directly to nominee outside of the deceased's estate; bypasses probate |
| Unit trust (through Public Mutual) | Nomination under CMSA | Nominee receives proceeds as a trustee for the estate; proceeds still form part of the estate for distribution |

This distinction matters enormously for estate planning speed. A PRS trust nomination can result in proceeds being paid within weeks; a unit trust nomination without a will or hibah structure means the balance enters the estate and is subject to probate timelines that can run 12–36 months in Malaysia.

### Four facts that matter for nomination and estate

- **A PRS nomination creates a trust relationship — the nominee receives the money as a beneficiary, not as a trustee for the estate.** This means PRS proceeds with a valid nomination pass outside the estate, are not subject to faraid distribution rules for non-Muslim estates, and are not frozen during probate. For Muslim members, the interaction with faraid is more nuanced (see below).
- **A unit trust nomination under CMSA does not pass outside the estate.** Despite the word "nomination", the unit trust nominee takes the proceeds as trustee — they are legally obliged to hold and distribute the money according to the terms of the estate. Without a will or hibah, the estate still needs to go through the grant of probate or letters of administration process.
- **For Muslim members, PRS trust nominations interact with faraid obligations.** Islamic estate law (faraid) governs the compulsory distribution of a Muslim's estate among eligible heirs. Whether a PRS trust nomination overrides faraid obligations or is treated as a voluntary gift (hibah) above the faraid entitlement is a question of syariah interpretation that varies by state. Members should obtain a specific ruling from a qualified religious authority (mufti or syariah court) rather than relying on generic advice.
- **Hibah (inter-vivos gift) is a separate instrument that can be layered alongside PRS.** A hibah is a gift completed during the giver's lifetime, which places the asset outside the estate at the time of the gift. Some PRS providers and unit trust companies facilitate hibah arrangements on their products. A hibah-structured PRS account passes to the hibah recipient directly, potentially bypassing both probate and faraid distribution — but only if structured and executed correctly. Legal and syariah advice is required.
- **Nomination records need to be kept current.** A nomination made at enrolment in 2010 and never updated may name an ex-spouse, a deceased parent, or a minor child who is now an adult. The PPA member portal allows nomination updates; members should review nominations at the same time they review their beneficiary designations on EPF and life insurance.

### Practical estate velocity

The primary practical benefit of understanding the nomination/hibah distinction is speed: a well-structured PRS nomination with a current nominee means a surviving spouse or child can receive the PRS balance in weeks rather than waiting out a full estate administration. For families with young children, that liquidity window in the months after a death matters enormously.

*Framing:* The nomination question is rarely raised in an initial PRS consultation. For analytical parents — especially those with young dependants or complex family structures — it is one of the highest-value conversations after the tax relief discussion. The framing is not morbid; it is "your nomination form is doing work whether you paid attention to it or not."
