# "It's too complicated, I won't understand it"

> Describes the small, fixed set of things a saver actually needs to read on a fund document; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"There are hundreds of funds, the documents are full of jargon, and I don't have a finance background. I'll never understand it well enough to choose, so I'd just be guessing — better to leave it alone."

### The true part

The overwhelm is real and the humility is healthy. There *are* hundreds of funds, the prospectus *is* long, and the jargon — NAV, alpha, Sharpe, asset allocation — is a genuine barrier for someone meeting it cold. A saver who refuses to put money into something they can't explain back is exercising exactly the right discipline; "I won't pretend to understand it" is a virtue, not a weakness.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is that **you don't read the whole prospectus to choose well — a fund's decision-relevant facts fit on one page, in a fixed shape** (corpus/knowledge/c-reading-a-factsheet.md). The Product Highlights Sheet and factsheet reduce every fund to the same small checklist: what the fund is *for* (its objective and who it suits), what it *holds* (the asset mix), how *risky* it is (a labelled risk band), what it *costs* (the fees), and how it has done *against its own benchmark* (alpha, not just the headline number). That is five questions, the same five for every fund, and answering them is closer to reading a nutrition label than doing the chemistry. The complexity that scares people lives in the machinery *behind* the fund, which the saver doesn't need to operate — the same way you can choose a car on a handful of specs without being able to build the engine. And the entire point of a licensed consultant is to do the translation: complexity is the consultant's job to carry, not a toll the saver must pay in confusion before they're allowed to start.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver being pushed to commit to something genuinely opaque — a structured product they can't summarise, a fund chosen for them with no explanation — the myth is the right alarm: don't buy what you can't have explained plainly. For the saver using "too complicated" to stay out entirely, it changes the answer: choosing a fund is a five-question, one-page exercise with a translator available, not a finance degree — and "I won't understand it" is a problem the factsheet and the consultant are built to solve, not a reason to forfeit decades of growth.
