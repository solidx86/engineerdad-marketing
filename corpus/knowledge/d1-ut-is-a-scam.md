# "UT is basically a scam like forex-MLM"

> Describes the regulated structure of Malaysian unit trusts versus unregulated schemes; states what is and isn't covered by deposit insurance; not personalised advice. Facts are dated regulatory facts from corpus/data/datasets/regulatory-facts.json.

## English

### The myth

"It's the same as those forex robots and MLM 'investment' schemes my relative lost money in — someone promises returns, takes your money, and one day it's gone. Unit trust is just a fancier version of the same con."

### The true part

The instinct that saves people here is correct: the schemes the myth is pattern-matching to — unlicensed forex 'managed accounts', money-game MLMs, fixed-payout 'investment' pools — are genuinely how people get robbed, and the warning signs they share (a pledge that the money can only go up, pressure to recruit, money paid to an individual's account) are exactly the right things to run from. Anyone who keeps that alarm is safer for it.

### The variable it ignores

The variable it ignores is **the regulatory and custody structure that a licensed unit trust sits inside, which the scams do not have** (corpus/data/datasets/regulatory-facts.json). A Public Mutual fund is approved and regulated by the Securities Commission Malaysia; it is distributed under FIMM, the industry self-regulatory body that licenses every consultant and can strike them off. Critically, the fund's assets are not held by the manager — they are held by an independent **trustee/custodian**, so the company managing the fund cannot simply withdraw and disappear with the money; that separation of manager from asset-holder is the single structural feature a Ponzi cannot replicate. The honest part of the comparison is the reverse of the myth: the tell of a scam is a sure-thing payout that supposedly cannot fall, and a regulated fund is barred from claiming any such certainty precisely because its returns are real and therefore uncertain. One caveat belongs in the same breath: this structure protects against fraud and misappropriation, not against market loss — a unit trust is **not** covered by PIDM deposit insurance (corpus/data/datasets/regulatory-facts.json), so the NAV can still fall. Regulated is not the same as safe; it is the difference between an honest risk and a con.

### Who it changes the answer for

For someone who was genuinely burned by a money game, the myth is protecting them from the right thing — and the answer isn't "trust everything", it's learning the one structural test: is there an SC licence, a named trustee holding the assets, and an absence of any claim that returns are certain. For the saver using "it's all a scam" to dismiss the entire regulated industry, it flips the answer: the scam pattern and the regulated-fund pattern are structurally opposite, and confusing them costs decades of legitimate compounding out of misplaced caution.
