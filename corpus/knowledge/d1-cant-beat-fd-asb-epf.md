# "UT can't even beat FD/ASB/EPF"

> A category-error correction, not a returns ranking. Acknowledges EPF's statutory guaranteed-minimum dividend and ASB's principal stability; never claims a unit trust is safer than or beats EPF/ASB. Figures dated; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"Why take market risk in a unit trust when ASB pays around 5.75% with no ups and downs, and EPF gave 6.3%? A fund can't even beat the guaranteed stuff — so it's pointless."

### The true part

This one is more right than most objections, and it has to be conceded plainly. ASB is **principal-stable** — its RM1.00 unit price doesn't fluctuate and it has no negative distribution year on record — and it paid roughly 5.0–5.75% over FY2021–2025 (corpus/data/datasets/asb-asnb-returns.json). EPF carries a **statutory guaranteed-minimum 2.5% dividend under the EPF Act 1991 §27** and declared 5.35–6.30% over 2022–2025 (corpus/data/datasets/epf-dividend-history.json). And over recent windows those returns genuinely **exceeded** broad Malaysian equity (the FBM KLCI's capital return was about 1.2% a year over five years). So the literal claim — that a capital-stable instrument out-returned the average local-equity index recently, at far lower risk — is true. A saver is not being foolish to prize the guaranteed tier.

### The variable it ignores

The error isn't the numbers; it's the **category** — comparing instruments that do different jobs as if they were rivals for one slot (corpus/data/charts/risk-return-positioning.yaml). FD, ASB and EPF are the capital-stable / statutory-floor tier; a unit trust is the market-risk tier; lining them up by a single return figure is the mistake, in either direction. The honest case for the fund tier is not "it beats ASB" — recently it didn't, and it is never *safer* than ASB or EPF. It is a *different job*: long-horizon growth potential and active alpha (the qualified Public Mutual equity universe ran a median weighted alpha of +2.04 above benchmark; corpus/proof/fund-universe-stats-snapshot.md) for money that can sit through deep drawdowns the guaranteed tier never has. Two structural ceilings matter too: ASB has restricted eligibility, and EPF money is locked until retirement and capped by your contributions — so neither can be the *whole* answer for, say, an education goal in 12 years. The point is not to move money out of the guaranteed tier; it is to recognise that the tier has a job it can't do, and that's the slot a fund fills.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver whose goal is short-horizon or who is eligible for ASB and content with a capital-stable ~5.5%, the myth is essentially correct — the guaranteed tier is the right home, and a fund would only add risk for no required return. For the saver who needs growth on long-horizon money beyond what EPF's capped, locked contributions and ASB's eligibility allow, it changes the answer: the question was never "which number is bigger" but "which job needs doing" — and matching the instrument to the horizon, not ranking them, is the literacy that the myth skips.
