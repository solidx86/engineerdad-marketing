# "EPF / the government will take care of me"

> Describes the mechanics and trade-offs of relying on EPF alone; all RM figures are illustrative model output or published KWSP guideline targets, dated, and not a forecast or guaranteed outcome. This is a balanced comparison; it makes no claim about the relative safety of any product versus EPF and no claim of outperforming EPF. Nothing here is personalised advice — read the relevant Master Prospectus and Product Highlights Sheet (PHS) before investing.

## English

### The myth

"My EPF is being deducted every month and it grows every year. By the time I retire there'll be a big number in there. The government has this handled — I don't need to do anything extra."

### The true part

EPF is a genuinely strong foundation, and several things in this view are correct. Contributions are automatic and disciplined, the fund is professionally managed and well-diversified, and — importantly — EPF carries a **guaranteed minimum dividend of 2.5% per annum under Section 27 of the EPF Act 1991**, a legal floor that ordinary market investments do not have. EPF really is doing a job, and doing it well. The concession is real: EPF is the right base layer, and dismissing it would be wrong. The myth's only error is treating the base layer as the *whole* answer.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is the **gap between what an EPF projection reaches and what an inflation-adjusted retirement actually costs**. KWSP itself publishes the targets. Its Retirement Income Adequacy schedule sets age-60 levels of **Basic RM390,000, Adequate RM650,000, and Enhanced RM1,300,000** (corpus/data/charts/epf-baseline-tiers.yaml), with a year-by-year track to get there — by age 40 the Adequate line is already about RM149,000, and by 50 about RM339,000 (corpus/data/charts/epf-savings-by-age.yaml).

The honest question is not whether EPF invests for you — it does — but whether your EPF projection lands on the Adequate line. For many members, modelling says it does not. The shortfall cases show even a million-ringgit projection leaving a monthly gap once inflation is applied to the lifestyle bill: a balance projected to last only into the early 70s against a life expectancy of 80 (corpus/proof/epf-shortfall-cases.md). The way to read this is **EPF is one engine; an inflation-adjusted retirement is the bill; the gap between them is what voluntary contributions — PRS or unit-trust accumulation — are meant to top up to reach the Adequate line.** It is a complement that closes a gap, not a replacement, and carries no claim about relative safety versus EPF or about outperforming it.

Two caveats on the targets: the RIA figures are **KWSP guideline targets, not a guarantee**, and they are stated **in today's ringgit**. A retirement two decades out must be **inflation-adjusted upward**, which only widens the gap a top-up has to close. Projections here are illustrative model output, not personalised advice and not a forecast.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a member whose own EPF projection already clears the Adequate line for their actual lifestyle, EPF alone may indeed be enough, and adding more is a preference, not a necessity. For the larger group whose projection falls short of Adequate — especially anyone retiring far enough out that inflation lifts the real target higher — the answer flips: "the government has it handled" understates the bill, and the practical move is to keep EPF as the base and add a voluntary top-up to close the gap. Which instrument closes it, and by how much, is a question for a one-to-one consultation that audits the individual's full position.
