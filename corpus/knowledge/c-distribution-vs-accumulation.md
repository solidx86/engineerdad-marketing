# Distribution vs Accumulation: When to Choose an Income-Paying Fund

> Figures reflect publicly published PMB distribution policies and SC guidelines as of 2026-05-31; consultant to confirm fund-specific distribution policy and history against the current PHS before use in campaign copy. Distributions are not guaranteed and a fund's distribution rate is not a return figure. Nothing here constitutes personalised advice or a recommendation to invest in any specific fund.

## English

This entry is the *selection* decision — **should you hold a distribution-paying fund at all** — not the mechanics of how a distribution is processed. For the ex-date NAV drop, reinvestment, and "distribution ≠ profit" mechanics, see `a-distribution-mechanics.md`. The two are a pair: mechanics explains *what happens*, this explains *which to pick and when*.

### The actual decision

The choice is not "which fund performs better." A growth/accumulation fund and an income/distribution fund can hold near-identical assets — the difference is what the fund *does with the income it earns*:

- **Accumulation / growth fund** — retains income and reinvests it inside the fund. Your return shows up as NAV appreciation. Nothing lands in your bank account until you redeem.
- **Distribution / income fund** — pays income out periodically (the NAV drops by the distributed amount on ex-date; see `a-distribution-mechanics.md`). Your return arrives partly as cash in hand.

The right answer is governed by **one question: when do you need the money?** — not by which has the bigger headline number.

### Life-stage mapping

- **Accumulation phase (a young parent saving for a child's education 10–18 years out):** an accumulation fund, or a distribution fund set to auto-reinvest, is almost always the rational default. The entire portfolio keeps compounding; nothing is pulled out to sit idle in a current account earning nothing.
- **Drawdown / income phase (a parent or grandparent who needs regular cash flow):** a distribution fund paid to a bank account makes practical sense — it produces recurring cash without the investor having to time and execute manual redemptions. The trade-off is that the unit-value base shrinks each time cash is paid out rather than reinvested.
- **A recurring near-term expense (e.g. funding annual school fees that start soon):** a distribution fund can match cash-flow timing to the expense — but only if the distribution is funded by genuine income, not by returning capital (see the trap below).

### The "distribution out of capital" trap

This is the sharp one, and the reason a high distribution rate can be a warning rather than a feature.

A fund's distribution is *supposed* to be paid out of the income and realised gains it actually earned. But SC guidelines permit, under disclosed conditions, **distribution out of capital** — paying out more than the fund earned in the period, with the shortfall funded by returning investors' own capital.

When that happens:

- The headline distribution rate looks attractive, but part of the "payout" is just your own money handed back to you.
- The NAV is permanently eroded beyond the normal ex-date mechanics — the fund is shrinking its own asset base to sustain a payout it isn't earning.
- An investor reading only the distribution percentage, not the source, can mistake capital erosion for yield.

The check: compare the distribution to the fund's actual income/return over the same period (the factsheet and prospectus disclose distribution policy and whether distributions may be made out of capital). A distribution materially exceeding what the fund earned is a flag, not a selling point.

### Four things most investors don't know

- **A "distribution fund" and a "growth fund" holding the same assets deliver the same total return — they differ only in whether income is paid out or retained.** Picking the distribution version does not add return; it changes the *form* of the return (cash vs NAV growth). Choosing one over the other on the basis of "which earns more" is a category error.
- **A high distribution rate is not a high return.** Distribution rate is income paid ÷ NAV; it says nothing about capital gains or losses. A fund can distribute 6% while its NAV falls 10% — the holder is poorer despite the "yield."
- **Some funds distribute out of capital — paying you back your own money and calling it a distribution.** SC rules permit this with disclosure; it permanently shrinks the NAV beyond the ordinary ex-date drop. The only way to catch it is to compare the payout against what the fund actually earned, which the prospectus distribution policy discloses.
- **For a parent in the accumulation phase, choosing a cash-paying distribution fund can quietly cost compounding.** Cash that lands in a current account earning ~0% is no longer participating in market growth; unless that cash is immediately redeployed, the distribution fund underperforms the same fund set to reinvest — not because of returns, but because of the idle-cash drag.
- **The behavioural axis matters as much as the financial one.** Auto-reinvestment enforces discipline (the money never tempts you to spend it); a cash payout creates a recurring decision to redeploy or spend. For a long-horizon education goal, the enforced-discipline default usually wins.

*Framing:* The strongest version of this for the analytical parent is the "distribution out of capital" reveal — the realisation that a fund can pay a generous-looking distribution while quietly returning your own capital and shrinking. It reframes "which fund pays the most?" into "where is that payout actually coming from?" — a question that signals the consultant is protecting the client from a yield illusion, not selling one.
