# "UT fees are high, ETF TER is ~0%"

> A cost-only comparison of two differently-structured routes; concedes the ETF's genuine cost advantage and is explicit this is not a suitability ranking. Figures dated; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"A unit trust charges me 1.7% a year plus a 5% entry load. An S&P 500 ETF charges 0.09%. The fund is robbing me — the ETF is basically free, same market exposure."

### The true part

This is largely correct and the brand does not pretend otherwise. On the headline expense ratio, an ETF wins decisively: SPY's TER is **0.0945%** and QQQ's is **0.18%**, against a Public Mutual global-equity fund's **1.76%/yr** management-plus-trustee fee, with a one-off sales charge on top (corpus/data/datasets/etf-cost-comparison.json). Quantified all-in over a 10-year hold, the DIY-ETF route comes to about **0.39%/yr** versus the unit trust's **~2.24%/yr** — the ETF really is several times cheaper, and anyone who tells a saver the costs are comparable is wrong. The cost gap is real and large.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is **the costs that don't appear in the TER — and the structural differences that the fee comparison silently drops**. Three frictions sit on the DIY-ETF side and not in its 0.09% sticker (corpus/data/datasets/etf-cost-comparison.json): US dividends paid to a Malaysian resident are docked **30% withholding tax with no US–Malaysia treaty relief** (about a 0.3%/yr drag on an S&P fund's yield); MYR↔USD **conversion cost is paid twice**, on the way in and out, and is embedded in the dealing rate rather than published; and US-situs holdings above **USD 60,000 expose the estate to US estate tax** (Form 706-NA, no treaty relief) — a tail risk a Malaysian-domiciled fund simply doesn't carry. None of this closes the cost gap — the ETF is still cheaper after counting them — so the honest turn is *not* "the UT is actually cheaper." It is that "basically free" overstates the ETF and "same exposure" is false: you're comparing a MYR-denominated, advised, locally-custodied, Shariah-available wrapper to a self-managed USD asset with withholding, FX and estate frictions and no advice layer. The fee buys a different structure, not the identical thing at a markup.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a cost-focused, self-directed saver with a long horizon, comfortable managing a US brokerage account and the tax/FX/estate admin, the myth is essentially right — the ETF's cost advantage is genuine and may well dominate, and that's a legitimate choice. For the saver who reads "ETF TER ≈ 0%" as the whole cost story, it changes the answer: the true DIY cost includes withholding, double FX and estate exposure, and the two routes aren't the same instrument — the cheaper one is also the one with no advice and more moving parts to get wrong.
