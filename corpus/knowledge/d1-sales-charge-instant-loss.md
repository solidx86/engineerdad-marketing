# "Sales charge = I start down 5%, guaranteed loss"

> Describes how a one-off entry charge is spread across the holding period; the channel-by-channel comparison lives in the related substitution entry. Figures are dated prospectus rates; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"The moment I buy, they take 5% off the top. So I'm starting at a guaranteed -5% — down before I even begin. That's a loss they've locked in on day one."

### The true part

The arithmetic of day one is real and shouldn't be waved away: pay an up-front sales charge and your invested amount on the first morning is below what you handed over. Two honest refinements before the turn — the headline rate is "of NAV per unit", so the agent/branch maximum of 5.0% of NAV works out to **4.76% of the gross amount paid** (RM476.19 on RM10,000, via the prospectus 5/105 formula; corpus/knowledge/a-sales-charge-math.md), not RM500 — but yes, you begin below your outlay, and that gap is real money.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is **time — a one-off charge is not an annual one, so it amortises across the whole holding period**. "Down 5%" treats a single entry cost as if it recurred every year; it does not. Spread a one-time ~4.76%-of-gross charge across a 10-year holding and it is on the order of half a percentage point a year; across 20 years, roughly a quarter-point a year — and across a single year it would indeed be punishing, which is precisely why a sales-charged fund is a long-horizon instrument, not a trading chip. The charge is a fixed toll paid once at the door, and the longer you stay, the more thinly that toll spreads over the money's working life. It is not a "guaranteed loss" — a loss is what you have if the investment never grows past the charge, and the entire point of matching the instrument to a long horizon is that compounding is expected to clear a one-off entry cost many times over. (What the charge *is* — and how much of it is avoidable by choosing a different channel — is a separate question, taken up in corpus/knowledge/d2-sales-charge-robbery.md; this entry is only about the day-one-loss framing.)

### Who it changes the answer for

For someone planning to buy and sell within a year or two, the myth is almost right: over that horizon a one-off entry charge genuinely can dominate the return, and a sales-charged fund is the wrong tool — a different channel or a different instrument fits better. For the saver with a 10-to-20-year goal who is refusing to start because of the day-one number, it changes the answer: a once-paid toll spread over two decades is a small annual drag, not a -5% you "start down" and never recover.
