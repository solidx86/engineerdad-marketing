# "Just DCA the index, you can't beat it"

> Concedes that most active funds trail the index (SPIVA) and frames the real risk as investor behaviour, not product choice; cites named research, makes no forecast. Not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"The data's clear — most active managers lose to the index. So I'll just dollar-cost-average into a cheap index fund and forget it. No point paying for active management."

### The true part

The evidence the myth leans on is real, and the honest answer leads with it rather than dodging it. S&P's SPIVA U.S. Year-End 2025 scorecard found **79% of active large-cap U.S. funds underperformed the S&P 500** in 2025, and over long horizons the underperformance rate exceeds 80% across every market S&P measures (corpus/data/datasets/spiva-behaviour-gap.json). Low-cost, diversified, automatic index investing is a genuinely excellent default, and dollar-cost-averaging is a sound way to remove timing guesswork (corpus/data/charts/dca-vs-lump.yaml). For a disciplined saver, "DCA a cheap index and leave it alone" is hard to beat — and the brand says so plainly.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is **the gap between the fund's return and the investor's return — behaviour, not the product**. Plans don't usually fail; people abandon them. Morningstar's *Mind the Gap* (US 2025) found that over the 10 years to 31 Dec 2024 the **average invested dollar earned 1.2 percentage points a year less** than the very funds it was in — about **15% of the total return given up** to the timing of buys and sells (corpus/data/datasets/spiva-behaviour-gap.json). That behaviour gap is *larger than the fee difference the myth is optimising*. The hard part of "just DCA the index" is not picking the index; it's still contributing in the month the index is down 30%, and not selling at the bottom (corpus/data/charts/panic-sell-vs-hold.yaml) — which is exactly where most of the lost return goes. The honest turn isn't "active beats passive" — SPIVA says it usually doesn't. It's that the index doesn't protect you from yourself, and the value a consultant can add is behavioural: keeping the plan funded and un-abandoned through the drawdowns where the behaviour gap is created.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a self-aware, automated saver who genuinely never flinches — keeps buying through crashes, never checks the balance in a panic — the myth is right, and a cheap index on autopilot is a superb plan that needs no help. For the saver who has bailed before, or who suspects they would, it changes the answer: the measurable risk to their outcome is the 1.2%/yr behaviour gap, not the fund's expense ratio — and an accountability layer that keeps them invested is worth more than the cost it adds.
