# Sales Charge Math: Upfront vs Annual-fee Drag Over Time

> Figures sourced from the Public Mutual Master Prospectus dated 28 August 2023 (with 1st Supplemental dated 3 December 2025) and PEF PHS dated 30 January 2026. Worked examples use round, illustrative numbers and stated assumptions — they are not personalised projections and not a forecast of any fund's actual return. All calculations assume a constant gross annual return for simplicity; real returns are variable. Verify against the current PHS for the specific fund before use in campaign copy or client advice.

## English

### The question the worked example answers

A 5.0% sales charge feels large. An annual management fee of 1.5% p.a. feels small. Over a 20-year holding period, which one costs more in absolute terms? The answer surprises most investors: **the recurring annual fee almost always dominates on long horizons**.

### Worked example — RM10,000 invested, 7% gross annual return assumption

*Assumptions (all illustrative): gross annual return 7%, AMF 1.5% p.a. reflected in net return (sourced: PEF AMF = 1.50% p.a., confirmed in PEF PHS dated 30 January 2026 and Master Prospectus p. 153; 1.50% is the AMF for domestic equity funds including PEF, PSF, PGF, PIX, and others), sales charge 5.0% upfront (confirmed: Master Prospectus p. 150 — "Up to 5.0% of NAV per unit" for equity/mixed-asset/balanced funds), holding periods 5 / 10 / 20 years. No tax, no reinvestment of distributions for simplicity. Figures are rounded to nearest RM.*

**Setup:**
- Gross investment: RM10,000
- Sales charge computation (from Master Prospectus p. 157 worked example): Sales Charge Incurred = Investment Amount × Sales Charge% ÷ (1 + Sales Charge%) = RM10,000 × 5.0% ÷ 1.05 = **RM476.19**; Net Investment Amount entering the fund = **RM9,523.81**
- Net annual return (after 1.5% AMF already deducted in NAV): 7.0% − 1.5% = 5.5% net

| Holding period | End value (net of AMF, post-charge entry) | Hypothetical end value if zero AMF, same entry | Cumulative AMF drag vs zero-AMF | Sales charge cost (RM476) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5 years | ~RM12,450 | ~RM13,370 | ~RM920 | RM476 |
| 10 years | ~RM16,280 | ~RM18,750 | ~RM2,470 | RM476 |
| 20 years | ~RM27,810 | ~RM36,900 | ~RM9,090 | RM476 |

*Note: "zero-AMF" scenario uses 7.0% net (same gross, no fee drag) applied to the same RM9,523.81 entry. Entry charge is RM476 in all scenarios (computed per the prospectus formula). All figures are illustrative outputs from confirmed rates.*

**What the table shows:** At year 5, the AMF drag (~RM920) already exceeds the one-time sales charge (RM476). At year 10, the AMF drag (~RM2,470) is roughly 5× the one-time charge. At year 20, the AMF drag (~RM9,090) is roughly 19× the upfront sales charge. **The break-even point — where cumulative AMF drag equals the sales charge — occurs at roughly 3–4 years under these assumptions.**

*Note on the previous version of this file: the earlier worked example used "RM9,500 enters the fund" (implying the charge is computed as 5.0% × RM10,000 = RM500 flat). The Master Prospectus p. 157 shows the exact formula: the charge is computed as RM10,000 × 5.0/105 = RM476.19, with RM9,523.81 entering the fund. The difference is small but the prospectus formula is correct.*

### The break-even insight

For a holding period longer than roughly 3–4 years (at these rates), the annual fee costs more in total than the one-time sales charge. This is not an argument that sales charges don't matter — RM476 is real money and compounds as lost capital. It is an argument that fee conversations must include the AMF, not just the upfront number.

Practical implication: an investor who switches funds every 2–3 years to "avoid" the recurring fee by chasing lower-AMF products will repeatedly reset the sales charge clock, potentially paying more in total charges than a long-term holder in a higher-AMF fund.

### Four things most investors don't know

- **On a 20-year horizon, the 1.5% p.a. AMF drag can cost roughly 19× more than the 5.0% one-time sales charge** (illustrative, at 7% gross return on RM10,000; both rates sourced from official documents — 5.0% from Master Prospectus p. 150, 1.50% AMF from PEF PHS 30 Jan 2026). The upfront number is more emotionally salient — but the recurring fee is the larger cumulative cost.
- **The break-even between upfront and annual fee drag occurs around 3–4 years under typical PMB equity-fund assumptions.** Hold longer than that and the AMF has already cost more than the entry charge. This is why long-horizon investors should understand the sales charge but focus heavily on the AMF.
- **A one-time 5.0% charge on a 20-year position amortises to roughly 0.25% per year of holding** (5.0% ÷ 20). On that basis it is cheaper per year than the AMF alone. Frame the upfront charge as a 20-year cost per year rather than a one-time shock.
- **Frequent switching resets the sales-charge clock and can destroy the long-horizon amortisation advantage.** An investor who switches equity funds every 3 years pays a new 5.0% charge each cycle — over 20 years that is ~6–7 entry charges, far exceeding a single 5.0% entry held to term.
- **Lower-AMF fund classes are meaningfully cheaper over long horizons even if they carry the same sales charge.** The prospectus lists AMFs ranging from 1.25% to 1.80% within the equity universe. A fund at 1.25% vs 1.80% represents a 0.55 percentage-point annual advantage — on RM10,000 at 7% gross over 20 years, the compounding difference in end value is in the thousands of ringgit.

### What this means for the client conversation

The fee conversation must happen in two steps: (1) the entry cost (sales charge), which is fixed and amortises with time; and (2) the running cost (AMF + trustee fee), which compounds against the investor every year. Clients who fixate only on the sales charge and ignore AMF differentials between fund options are optimising the wrong variable for a long holding period.

*Framing:* The maths here is simple enough to put on a single slide — but almost no retail investor has done it. Walking a client through the break-even table is the fastest way to shift the conversation from "how much do you charge to get in?" to "what does this cost me over the life of the investment?"
