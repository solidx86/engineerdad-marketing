# "QQQ/SPY crush Malaysian funds"

> Names the period shown, concedes the US market's genuine long-run strength, and never implies the MYR adjustment makes a unit trust "win"; the point is the ignored forex and concentration variables. Figures dated; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"Look at the numbers — QQQ did ~18% a year, SPY ~14%, while Malaysian funds and the KLCI went nowhere. Why would I buy local when the US indices crush everything?"

### The true part

The performance gap is real and large, and it survives the obvious rebuttal. Over the five years to May 2026, QQQ returned **17.9%/yr in USD and 16.99%/yr after the MYR adjustment**, SPY **14.0%/13.1%**; over ten years QQQ ran **21.8%/21.3%** (corpus/data/datasets/us-vs-my-returns.json) — against the FBM KLCI's **1.2%/yr capital return** over five years. The MYR move was small over these windows, so "but currency eats it" does *not* rescue the local comparison: even in ringgit terms, US indices massively outperformed. The brand concedes this without flinching — the US market's long-run strength is genuine, and recency hasn't been kind to Malaysian equity.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variables are **the ones a backward-looking winner's chart hides: forex direction, single-country concentration, and what currency you actually spend in**. First, the MYR adjustment was benign over *these* windows; it is not a law — a different decade with a strengthening ringgit subtracts from the USD return, and the dataset keeps both the 5y and 10y standard periods precisely because different windows give different answers (corpus/data/charts/usd-return-vs-myr-adjusted.yaml). Second, QQQ's ~20% decade is a *single-country, tech-concentrated* outcome (corpus/data/charts/single-country-vs-global.yaml); buying it as your core is a bet that the last decade's most concentrated winner keeps winning — the exact recency error SPIVA-style evidence warns against. Third, a Malaysian parent's liabilities — school fees, a home, retirement spending — are in **ringgit**, so a 100%-USD portfolio adds a currency mismatch to the goal that the headline return ignores. The honest turn is not "the unit trust wins" — on these windows it didn't, and pretending the forex adjustment flips the ranking would be dishonest. It's that "crush" is a past-tense, single-country, single-currency number, and building a 20-year plan on it imports concentration and forex risk the chart never priced.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver with a long horizon, USD-aware, who genuinely wants concentrated US exposure and can stomach a currency mismatch, the myth points at a real opportunity — US indices were the place to be, and direct exposure is a legitimate choice (with the cost and structure caveats in the related ETF entries). For the saver about to put their whole ringgit-denominated education fund into last decade's hottest single-country index because it "crushes" everything, it changes the answer: that's a recency-and-forex bet dressed as an obvious win — diversification across markets and currencies is the variable the winner's chart left out.
