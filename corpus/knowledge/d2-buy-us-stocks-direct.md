# "I'll buy US stocks/ETFs direct for nearly free"

> A cost-and-structure comparison; concedes the direct route's low headline cost and is explicit about the frictions it omits. Figures dated; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"I'll open a US brokerage account and buy SPY or the stocks directly — commissions are near zero now, the TER is 0.09%, so I get the real thing for almost nothing instead of paying a fund."

### The true part

The headline is genuinely cheap and the brand concedes it. SPY's expense ratio is **0.0945%**, broker commissions on US trades run around **0.1% (min ~USD 3)**, and the quantified DIY all-in over a 10-year hold is about **0.39%/yr** — far below a unit trust's ~2.24%/yr (corpus/data/datasets/etf-cost-comparison.json). For a hands-on saver, direct US exposure really is a low-cost way to own the market, and "nearly free" is close to true *on the visible line items*.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variables are **the frictions and the tail risk that don't show up in a TER** (corpus/data/charts/true-cost-stack-ut-vs-etf.yaml). Three of them sit only on the direct route: (1) US dividends to a Malaysian resident are docked **30% withholding tax with no US–Malaysia treaty to reduce it** — a permanent leak on the income portion that no expense ratio captures; (2) the **MYR↔USD conversion is paid twice**, in and out, embedded in the dealing rate rather than published, so "nearly free" silently excludes a real recurring cost; and (3) the one most people never price — **US estate tax**. US-situs assets (US-listed shares and ETFs) above **USD 60,000** at death expose a Malaysian's estate to US estate tax via **Form 706-NA, with no treaty relief** — roughly RM244,000 of holdings is the threshold, and above it the estate faces a US filing obligation and potential tax a Malaysian-domiciled fund route never triggers (corpus/data/datasets/etf-cost-comparison.json). None of this makes the unit trust cheaper — the direct route still wins on cost — so the honest turn is not "the fund is a better deal." It's that "nearly free" omits withholding and double FX, and "direct" quietly takes on a US estate-tax exposure and an admin/behaviour burden that a long-horizon family should price in before treating it as a no-brainer.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a cost-driven, self-directed saver who will hold under the estate threshold or has planned around it, manage the FX and withholding, and stay the course alone, the myth is largely right — direct ETF exposure is cheap and legitimate. For the saver building a large, multi-decade US holding "for nearly free" with no estate plan, it changes the answer: above USD 60,000 of US-situs assets the estate exposure is a real, uninsured tail risk, and the withholding and double FX mean the true cost is higher than the 0.09% sticker — cheaper than a fund, but not free, and not without strings.
