# "Past performance means nothing, it's all luck"

> Describes what a factsheet's history does and doesn't tell you; treats past performance as a backward-looking signal, never a forecast. No future-performance promise; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"Every fund document literally says 'past performance is not indicative of future results.' So the track record is meaningless — picking a fund on history is just picking last year's lucky number."

### The true part

The disclaimer is true and load-bearing, not legal boilerplate to ignore: **past performance does not predict future returns**, a fund that led last year can lag next year, and chasing the single top number is a known way to buy high. The myth is also right that for a single year, noise dominates — one good year is mostly luck, and reading it as skill is the error the disclaimer is built to prevent. Anyone who keeps that skepticism avoids the most common retail mistake.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is that **"past performance" is not one number — a factsheet carries a process signal, not just a return** (corpus/knowledge/c-reading-a-factsheet.md). The headline return is the least informative line on the page; what a careful reader takes from history is *consistency across periods*, behaviour in down-markets (drawdown and recovery), the risk taken to earn the return, and — the metric the brand leans on — **alpha**, the return above the fund's own benchmark, which separates "the whole market went up" from "this manager added value." A long, repeated record of positive benchmark-relative alpha across different conditions is weak evidence about *next quarter* and meaningfully stronger evidence about *process* than a coin-flip would produce — which is the honest middle ground between "history predicts the future" (false) and "history is pure luck" (also false). The universe statistics make the point that persistence is partial, not absent (corpus/proof/fund-universe-stats-snapshot.md): it is a selection signal to weigh, not a guarantee to bank on, and it is read alongside cost and fit, never alone.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver about to buy this year's chart-topper *because* it topped the chart, the myth is the right corrective — that single number is mostly luck and the disclaimer means it. For the saver using "it's all luck" to refuse any evidence-based selection at all, it changes the answer: a track record read for consistency, drawdown behaviour and benchmark-relative alpha is a legitimate process signal — backward-looking and uncertain, but not meaningless, and not a forecast.
