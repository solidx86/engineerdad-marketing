# The Bid-Offer Spread Is Retired: Single-NAV Pricing in Modern PMB Funds

> Figures and pricing descriptions reflect PMB's current single-pricing framework as of 2026-05-31. Older prospectuses, third-party articles, and comparison sites may still reference bid-offer spreads for PMB funds — treat any such reference as outdated unless confirmed against a current PMB prospectus or PHS. Consultant to verify pricing model for any specific fund before use in campaign copy.

## English

### What the bid-offer spread was

In the historic dual-pricing model used by many Malaysian unit trust funds (and still used in some markets globally), two prices were published for every fund:

- **Offer price (buy price):** the price at which an investor purchased units — typically higher than NAV, embedding the sales charge plus initial expenses.
- **Bid price (sell price):** the price at which an investor redeemed units — typically the NAV or slightly below, embedding distribution costs.

The **bid-offer spread** was the difference between these two prices. On a typical equity fund with a 5.0% sales charge (illustrative; historically figures varied — current verified ceiling is 5.0%), an offer price of RM1.050 and a bid price of RM1.00 represented a spread of 5.0%. The spread was the mechanism for collecting the sales charge — it was built into the pricing itself rather than disclosed as a separate line item.

### The single-NAV transition

Public Mutual, in line with SC/FIMM regulatory direction, moved to **single-pricing** (also called single-NAV pricing). Under this model:

- There is only one published price per fund per business day: the NAV.
- The sales charge is disclosed and collected **separately** — as an explicit percentage of the gross investment amount, not embedded in a spread between two prices.
- The redemption price equals the NAV (no bid discount).

**On a modern PMB statement, you will not see an offer price column and a bid price column. You will see a single NAV.**

### Why older references still mention a spread

Several contexts still produce bid-offer spread language:

1. **Older marketing materials and factsheets** that have not been fully updated.
2. **Third-party comparison portals and financial media** that pre-date the single-pricing transition or use legacy data.
3. **Older investors or agents** who learned the product under the dual-pricing era and still use the terminology habitually.
4. **Historical prospectuses** held by investors who invested years ago and have not read a current edition.

None of these represent a current PMB pricing fact. Any current PMB prospectus or PHS will confirm single-NAV pricing.

### What the investor sees on a modern PMB statement

| Statement column | What it shows |
|---|---|
| Units held | Number of units held in the fund |
| NAV per unit | Single published price per unit (post-AMF, post-trustee fee) |
| Market value | Units × NAV — the current value of the holding |
| Cost / invested | Gross amount the investor paid (including sales charges at time of purchase) |

There is no "offer price" or "bid price" column. The sales charge paid at entry is a historical cost, not a live pricing spread. If an older statement shows two prices, it was issued under the prior regime.

### Four things most investors don't know

- **PMB no longer uses a bid-offer spread.** The fund has a single NAV at which both purchases and redemptions are transacted. Any article or comparison tool that shows a PMB bid-offer spread is citing outdated information.
- **Under the old dual-pricing system, the spread was the sales charge in disguise.** Investors who bought at the offer price and redeemed at the bid price effectively paid the spread as their entry cost — but it was not as legibly disclosed as the current explicit percentage-of-gross-investment model. The shift to single pricing improved transparency, not economics.
- **The modernisation means the sales charge is now a fully explicit, separately disclosed cost.** You can see exactly what percentage is charged, exactly how much of your investment is deducted, and exactly how many units you receive at the post-charge amount. There is no hidden embedding in a price spread.
- **A "0% bid-offer spread" claim on a modern PMB product is technically true but misleading if it implies no charges.** The sales charge still exists — it is just collected transparently as a separate line rather than hidden in a spread. Zero spread ≠ zero charges; it means the charges are disclosed rather than embedded.

### Practical implication for the client conversation

When a prospective investor shows you an older analysis or comparison tool that cites a PMB bid-offer spread, the correct response is: "That is the old pricing model — PMB moved to single-NAV pricing under SC guidelines. The sales charge still exists, but it is now shown as an explicit upfront deduction rather than a spread between two prices. The economic effect is the same; the transparency is better."

This framing:
1. Does not dismiss the investor's research effort.
2. Accurately corrects the outdated claim.
3. Does not pretend charges don't exist.

*Framing:* The bid-offer spread question is a diagnostic for how current an investor's knowledge is. An investor asking about "the spread" is reading older material — and the consultant who can clearly explain the transition from dual to single pricing, and why it matters for transparency, demonstrates exactly the kind of technical depth that differentiates a knowledgeable adviser from a brochure reciter.
