# "Agents just churn you for commission"

> Describes the mechanics of switching costs and the conduct rules a licensed consultant operates under; not a claim about any individual agent and not personalised advice. Figures are the published prospectus switching terms, dated.

## English

### The myth

"The agent makes money every time I move funds, so they'll keep telling me to switch — buy this, sell that, into the next 'hot' fund — and I'm the one who pays for their commission churn."

### The true part

The incentive is real and worth naming, not denying. A consultant is paid on the business they place, so a conflict of interest exists in principle — the same conflict that exists for any commissioned salesperson. A saver is right to watch for it, right to ask "why this switch, why now," and right to walk away from anyone who can't answer in terms of the saver's goal rather than the product. Skepticism here is healthy, not paranoid.

### The variable it ignores

What the myth ignores is that **churning is expensive and constrained, not free money**. Switching between Public Mutual funds is not costless: the switching matrix (corpus/knowledge/a-switching-matrix.md) sets out the per-switch fees and the cases where a switch crosses fund families and re-triggers a sales-charge differential — the double-charge trap walked through in corpus/knowledge/a-scenario-double-charged.md. Every unnecessary switch a consultant pushes leaves a visible cost trail on the client's own statement, which is the opposite of a quiet skim. On top of the economics, a FIMM-licensed consultant operates under a Code of Ethics that requires suitability and prohibits churning for commission as a disciplinable offence — the licence itself is the asset they would be risking. The honest frame is not "agents can't have a conflict" but "the structure makes visible, repeated churn a bad trade for the agent too."

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver who has actually been switched repeatedly into this year's top performer with no goal-based reason, the myth is describing a real bad actor — and the fix is to ask for the switch's cost and rationale in writing, or change consultant, not to avoid unit trusts. For the saver using "agents just churn" as a blanket reason to never start, it changes the answer: the conflict is manageable with two questions (what does this switch cost me, and how does it serve my goal), and a switch you initiate for a genuine reallocation is a normal, cheap tool — not evidence of a scam.
