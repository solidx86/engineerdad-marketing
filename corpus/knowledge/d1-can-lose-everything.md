# "You can lose everything — it's gambling"

> Describes how diversification and risk tiers shape the loss a unit-trust holder is actually exposed to, and is explicit that funds carry real, uninsured market risk; not personalised advice. Drawdown figures are dated universe statistics.

## English

### The myth

"Putting money in a fund is just gambling with extra steps. The market crashes and you can lose the whole thing — same as betting it all on one stock or one hand of cards."

### The true part

The refusal to pretend investing is safe is the right instinct, and the honest answer concedes the core of it: a unit trust carries **real market risk and is not capital-guaranteed**. It is not a deposit — it is **not covered by PIDM** (corpus/data/datasets/regulatory-facts.json), the NAV moves down as well as up, and the drawdowns are not hypothetical. Across the Public Mutual universe the median fund sits about -3.93% below its own all-time-high NAV and the deepest drawdown on file is -49.32% (corpus/proof/fund-universe-stats-snapshot.md). Anyone who tells a saver a fund can't fall hard is lying; the myth is right that the downside is real.

### The variable it ignores

What separates it from gambling is **diversification and a defined risk tier, which change the shape of the loss** (corpus/knowledge/c-risk-metric-glossary.md). A single bet has a binary, total-loss outcome; a diversified equity fund holds dozens of companies, so "lose everything" is not a mechanic that exists short of every underlying company simultaneously going to zero — which is not what a market crash is. A crash is a deep, temporary drawdown of a still-positive pile, not a zeroing. The risk-metric glossary also makes the point that a fund's risk is a *chosen, labelled* variable: a money-market or bond fund and an aggressive equity fund are different tiers doing different jobs, and a saver picks the tier that matches the horizon. Gambling has a negative expected value by design; a diversified fund matched to a long horizon has a positive expected value with interim volatility — the same volatility the deepest-drawdown figure measures, recovered by time, not by luck. The risk is real; the "everything, like a casino" framing is the part that's wrong.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver about to put short-term money — next year's fees, the emergency fund — into an aggressive equity fund, the myth is pointing at a real mistake: that money can absolutely fall when it's needed, and it belongs in the capital-stable tier instead. For the saver using "it's all gambling" to keep a 20-year education fund in cash, it changes the answer: matched to its horizon, a diversified fund's deep-but-temporary drawdowns are a risk that time is designed to absorb — refusing the tier entirely is its own quiet loss.
