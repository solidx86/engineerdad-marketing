# Currency Exposure in PMB Global and Regional Funds

> Figures reflect publicly published PMB factsheets as of 2026-05-31; fund-specific values change quarterly — verify against the current factsheet. Not personalised advice.

## English

### The hidden bet inside every non-domestic PMB fund

When a Malaysian investor buys a PMB global equity fund, they make two investments simultaneously: an investment in the underlying portfolio of foreign equities, and a **currency bet** — specifically, a bet that the MYR will not appreciate against the USD, EUR, or other currencies in which those equities are denominated. Most factsheets disclose this risk in the risk section; few investors read it carefully enough to understand how large the currency effect can be relative to the equity-market effect.

---

### How currency exposure enters the portfolio

For a typical unhedged PMB global equity fund:

1. The fund manager selects equities denominated in USD, EUR, GBP, JPY, or other currencies.
2. The fund's NAV is calculated in MYR.
3. When the fund converts foreign-currency asset values to MYR for NAV calculation, the prevailing exchange rate applies.
4. If the MYR strengthens against USD (e.g., from 4.70 to 4.40 per USD), the same USD-denominated portfolio is worth **fewer MYR** when converted — a capital loss to the MYR-based investor with no change in the underlying equity values.
5. Conversely, if MYR weakens (e.g., from 4.40 to 4.80 per USD), the portfolio gains MYR value regardless of equity market movement.

The currency translation effect can be substantial. In years of large MYR/USD moves (e.g., the 2015–2016 MYR depreciation, which saw the USD/MYR rate move from ~3.50 to ~4.50 at the trough), the FX component of return can exceed the equity alpha in magnitude.

---

### Where the factsheet shows currency exposure

**Geographic allocation table:** Shows the percentage of the portfolio in each country/region. For a global fund, this directly implies currency exposure — a 45% US allocation means approximately 45% of the portfolio is in USD-denominated assets (unless hedged; see below).

**Currency breakdown (where disclosed):** Some PMB factsheets include an explicit currency breakdown table (e.g., USD 42%, EUR 15%, JPY 8%, other 10%, MYR 25%). When this table is present, use it rather than inferring from geographic allocation, since some companies are listed in one country but generate revenues in a different currency.

**Risk section:** The prospectus and PHS for every regional or global PMB fund will include a currency risk disclosure paragraph. Reading this section identifies whether the fund uses any currency hedging and whether a hedged share class is available.

**What the factsheet buries or omits:** The factsheet typically does not show how much currency movement contributed to each period's return versus how much came from the equity portfolio's performance. Separating these requires either an FX-adjusted return calculation or a look at the fund's annual report where managers may disclose the FX attribution.

---

### Hedged vs unhedged share classes

Some PMB funds offer a **hedged** variant (where the MYR/foreign-currency exposure is reduced or eliminated via forward contracts) alongside the standard unhedged class.

| Feature | Unhedged class | Hedged class |
|---|---|---|
| Currency exposure | Full FX exposure to fund's currency mix | FX exposure substantially reduced |
| Cost | No explicit hedging cost | Hedging cost embedded in returns (cost depends on interest rate differential between MYR and the hedged currency) |
| Return in MYR | USD/MYR moves directly affect MYR NAV | MYR NAV more closely tracks the fund's foreign-currency-denominated performance |
| When advantaged | When MYR depreciates vs fund currencies | When MYR appreciates vs fund currencies (or when the hedging cost is low) |
| PMB availability | Standard for most PMB global/regional funds | Not universal — check whether the specific fund offers a hedged class |

**The hedging cost mechanism:** Hedging via FX forwards is not free. The cost reflects the interest rate differential between MYR and the foreign currency being hedged. When MYR interest rates are materially lower than USD rates (which has been the historical norm), the MYR hedged class bears a carry cost that reduces returns relative to the unhedged class in a stable-FX environment. When MYR appreciates, the hedged class outperforms the unhedged class net of cost.

---

### How FX can swamp underlying alpha

An illustrative (not fund-specific) scenario:

- A PMB global equity fund's underlying portfolio of USD-denominated equities returns +10% in USD terms over a calendar year.
- Over the same year, MYR appreciates from 4.70 to 4.30 against USD — an 8.5% MYR appreciation.
- The MYR-denominated NAV return for the unhedged fund would be approximately +10% − 8.5% = **+1.5%** in MYR terms.
- A Malaysian investor who expected a "global equity fund" to deliver equity-market returns in MYR might be surprised that a strong year for global equities produced a near-flat MYR return.

The reverse also applies: in a year when global equities are flat but MYR weakens significantly, the MYR-based investor receives an apparent "gain" from currency depreciation — a gain that does not reflect any underlying equity market performance.

*This scenario is illustrative. Actual fund returns depend on portfolio composition, hedging, and the specific FX rates applicable in the period.*

---

### The liability-currency matching principle

For a parent saving for a specific future liability, matching the liability's currency to the fund's currency exposure reduces FX risk. Examples:

- **Overseas university fees (USD/GBP):** A fund with high USD or GBP exposure (unhedged) functions as a natural hedge against the liability — if MYR weakens and fees get more expensive in MYR, the fund's MYR NAV also rises. However, this is an imperfect hedge since the fund's equity returns add volatility.
- **Local Malaysian expense (MYR):** A domestic equity fund eliminates FX risk entirely — returns are in MYR because all holdings are MYR-denominated.
- **Mixed liability (partly overseas, partly local):** A blended allocation across domestic and global funds creates a partial natural hedge. The optimal mix depends on the parent's estimate of future spending in each currency.

---

### Four things most investors get wrong about currency exposure

- **"Global fund" does not mean "global diversification from currency risk"** — an unhedged global fund priced in MYR is a concentrated bet on MYR weakening or holding flat relative to the fund's currency mix. Currency risk is not diversified away by buying a geographically diverse portfolio.
- **The published MYR NAV return already embeds the FX effect — there is no separate FX line.** An investor who sees +3% on their global fund statement in a year when global equities rose 12% may not realise that MYR appreciation consumed the other 9 percentage points. The factsheet return figure does not decompose currency vs equity contributions.
- **A hedged class is not always better.** The hedging cost is real and is deducted from the fund's return. In a stable-FX or MYR-depreciating environment, the unhedged class outperforms the hedged class after hedging costs. The hedged class is advantaged specifically when MYR appreciates, or when the investor's primary goal is to eliminate FX uncertainty rather than maximise MYR returns.
- **Regional funds have multi-currency exposure, not just USD.** A PMB Asia-Pacific fund may hold equities in JPY, KRW, TWD, HKD, SGD, and INR alongside USD. The MYR return is exposed to every one of those exchange rates relative to MYR, not just MYR/USD. Currency diversification within the portfolio reduces single-currency FX risk but does not eliminate it.

*Framing:* The currency conversation is the second thing to teach a parent who has decided to invest in a global or regional fund, right after the equity-risk conversation. An investor who does not understand currency exposure is likely to misattribute performance, hold the wrong fund for their liability, and make redemption decisions based on FX noise rather than underlying investment merit.
