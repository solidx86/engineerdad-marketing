# "Shariah funds are just marketing / underperform"

> Describes the screening methodology behind a Shariah fund and how its performance compares; not a religious ruling and not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"Slapping 'Shariah' on a fund is just a marketing label to attract Muslim money — underneath it's the same thing, and if anything it does worse because it can't invest in as much."

### The true part

There are two real points tangled together here, and both deserve a straight answer. First, the *constraint is real*: a Shariah fund genuinely cannot hold everything a conventional fund can — conventional banks, alcohol, gambling, tobacco and excessively-leveraged companies are screened out — so the investable universe is narrower, and in a period when the excluded sectors (say, conventional financials) lead the market, a Shariah fund can lag its conventional cousin. The myth is right that the screen has a performance *consequence*; it is not free. Second, skepticism toward any label used to sell is healthy.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is that **the screen is a governed methodology with a real economic effect, not a sticker — and over full cycles the performance gap runs both ways, not just down** (corpus/knowledge/c-shariah-vs-conventional.md). A Malaysian Shariah fund is screened against the Securities Commission's Shariah Advisory Council criteria — quantitative business-activity and financial-ratio thresholds, reviewed periodically — so "Shariah-compliant" is an audited classification, not a brand claim a manager can self-apply. That screen does more than exclude: by filtering out the most heavily-leveraged companies, it imposes a quality/leverage discipline that can *help* in some environments (credit stress, rate shocks) as much as it hurts in others (financials-led rallies). The honest summary is *comparable long-run risk and return from a different investable universe*, with cycle-dependent leads in either direction — not a built-in penalty. And for a saver to whom compliance matters, the choice isn't a performance trade at all; it's a non-negotiable filter that happens to cost little over a full cycle.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver with no compliance requirement who is comparing a specific Shariah fund to a specific conventional one over a window where the excluded sectors led, the myth can be locally true — that fund lagged, for a structural reason worth understanding. For the saver who *wants* compliance but is staying out because they assume "Shariah = marketing = worse", it changes the answer: the screen is a governed methodology with comparable full-cycle outcomes, so they can satisfy the requirement without resigning themselves to a penalty that the cycle-by-cycle record doesn't actually support.
