# PMB Fee Schedule

> Figures sourced from the Public Mutual Master Prospectus dated 28 August 2023 (with 1st Supplemental dated 3 December 2025) and individual Product Highlight Sheets (PHS) dated November 2025 – February 2026. Verify against the current PHS for the specific fund before use in campaign copy or client advice, as fees may be amended by supplemental prospectus. Sales charge rates may also vary by distributor and campaign period. Nothing here constitutes personalised advice or a recommendation to invest.

## English

### The PMB fee structure at a glance

PMB funds carry two categories of charges: **one-time transaction charges** (sales charge, switching fee, redemption fee) and **ongoing fund-level charges** (annual management fee, trustee fee). Understanding both categories prevents the common mistake of focusing only on the upfront number.

- **Redemption proceeds paid:** within 7 Business Days (4 Business Days for PMMF).
- **Cooling-off right:** first-time individual investors only; request must be submitted within 6 Business Days of receipt of application form and payment by Public Mutual. Refund = lower of original purchase price or current market price, plus the sales charge paid.

### Four things most investors don't know

- **Bond funds carry a materially lower sales charge than equity funds.** The ceiling for bond/fixed-income PMB funds is up to 1.0% (confirmed: PBBOND PHS, Master Prospectus p. 150), versus up to 5.0% for equity funds — the differential matters for clients using bonds as a short-to-medium-term holding.
- **Money-market funds carry zero sales charge.** An investor parking cash in PMMF pays no entry fee (confirmed: PMMF PHS, Master Prospectus p. 150); the full amount enters the fund immediately and begins accruing interest from the dealing day.
- **The annual management fee is already inside the published NAV — you will never receive an AMF invoice.** The fee accrues daily and is deducted from fund assets before the NAV is struck (Master Prospectus p. 154); the published NAV is always net of AMF and trustee fee. What you see in your statement is the post-fee value.
- **The trustee fee is a separate line from the AMF — and varies by fund class.** For equity funds it is 0.06% p.a.; for bond funds 0.035% p.a.; for money market 0.02% p.a. (all confirmed from prospectus p. 153 and individual PHS). Both the AMF and trustee fee are inside the NAV.
- **The sales charge is computed as 5% of the net sum that enters the fund, added on top — equivalent to dividing by 1.05.** The prospectus formula (p. 157 worked example): Sales Charge = Investment Amount × 5 / 105. On a RM10,000 investment: RM10,000 × 5/105 = **RM476.19 charge**, **RM9,523.81 net invested** (38,095.24 units at NAV RM0.25). The charge is 5% of the net amount, not 5% of the gross — RM500 is not the correct figure. The immediate market-to-book gap is the RM476.19 charge amount.

### Why the fee class matters for fund selection

The practical implication of the fee table: using an equity-class sales charge of 5.0% on a short-horizon position (say, 18 months) means the fund must return more than 5.0% just to break even on entry costs. On a 10–20 year horizon that same 5.0% amortises to under 0.3% per year of holding. Matching the fund class to the holding horizon is therefore a basic discipline — the fee schedule is not neutral information.

The AMF range within equity funds (1.25%–1.80% p.a.) is also material on long horizons: a 0.55 percentage-point difference between a 1.25% and 1.80% AMF fund compounds to a meaningful difference in end wealth over 20 years.

*Framing:* The fee table is the entry-level literacy test for any analytical investor. Consultants who can walk a client through every row — explaining why bond funds charge less, why the AMF doesn't appear as a line item, and what the trustee actually does — establish technical credibility before any performance conversation begins.
