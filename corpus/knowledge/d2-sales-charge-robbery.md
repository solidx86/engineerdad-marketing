# "5–5.5% sales charge is robbery"

> Frames the sales charge as a channel-dependent variable, not a fixed tax; the one-off-amortisation argument lives in the related avoidance entry. Figures dated; EPF caps subject to change by EPF; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"They take five percent of everything I put in. That's daylight robbery — a tax for the privilege of handing them my money."

### The true part

Five percent of your entry is real money, and on a large lump sum it stings — that part isn't spin. The maximum agent/branch sales charge on a Public Mutual equity fund genuinely is **up to 5.0% of NAV** (which is 4.76% of the gross amount paid, via the prospectus 5/105 formula; corpus/knowledge/a-sales-charge-math.md). A saver is right to treat a 5% entry cost as a serious number worth minimising, not waving away.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is that **the sales charge is a chosen variable that depends on the channel — not a fixed tax that's the same everywhere** (corpus/data/charts/sales-charge-by-channel.yaml). The same family of funds carries a *different* maximum charge depending on how you buy it: up to **5.0%** through an agent or branch (which buys a licensed consultant's service — suitability, paperwork, ongoing review); the same published cap self-serve through Public Mutual Online; **3%** through EPF-MIS via an agent; and as low as **0.5% per buy transaction** self-serve through EPF i-Invest (corpus/data/datasets/sales-charge-by-channel.json; KWSP figures, "subject to change by EPF"). So the charge buys *different things*, and a cost-sensitive saver has cheaper doors — the 5% is the full-service price, not an unavoidable levy. Two honest caveats keep this from becoming "just use i-Invest for free": the cheaper EPF channels are gated (members below 55, eligibility capped at 30% of savings above your age-band Basic Savings, money there stops earning the EPF dividend) — covered in corpus/knowledge/d2-just-use-epf-iinvest.md — and the *amortisation* point (a one-off charge spread over a long holding period is a small annual drag, not a recurring 5%) is argued in corpus/knowledge/d1-sales-charge-instant-loss.md. This entry's single job is to break the "fixed tax" framing: the charge is a dial, and you can turn it down by choosing the channel that matches how much service you need.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a confident, self-directed saver who doesn't need the consultant layer, the myth points at a real waste — paying 5% for service you won't use is the avoidable part, and a cheaper self-serve or EPF channel is the fix. For the saver calling the whole instrument "robbery" and staying out, it changes the answer: 5% is the price of one specific door, not the building — the charge is a chosen variable, and the honest question is which level of service is worth paying for, not whether the fund is a scam.
