# "My money gets locked in / hard to withdraw"

> Describes the redemption and settlement mechanics of an open-end unit trust; not personalised advice. Timing is the published prospectus process, dated.

## English

### The myth

"Once it's in a fund, it's stuck. Getting it out is a hassle, takes forever, and I can't touch it if I suddenly need cash — like a fixed deposit you can't break, or worse."

### The true part

The fear is reasonable because some savings vehicles genuinely *are* locked: EPF is inaccessible until retirement conditions are met, PRS withdrawals before 55 are penalised, and a fixed deposit broken early forfeits its interest. If a saver's mental model of "long-term savings" is built from those, expecting a unit trust to trap money the same way is a sensible default — and liquidity for emergencies is a real, correct priority.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is that **a unit trust is open-ended and redeemable on any business day — it is one of the more liquid long-term vehicles, not a locked one** (corpus/knowledge/a-nav-cutoff-settlement.md). You can submit a redemption for any amount on any dealing day; units are priced by *forward pricing* at that day's 4pm cut-off (a request in before 4pm gets that day's NAV, after 4pm the next day's), and the proceeds are paid out within the prospectus settlement window — days, not years, and with no penalty for leaving and no interest to forfeit. There is no fixed term to "break." The contrast with the vehicles the myth borrows from is the whole point: EPF and PRS trade liquidity for a retirement lock and a tax/dividend benefit; an ordinary unit trust does not impose that lock at all. The one honest caveat is *settlement is not instant and price is not fixed*: redeeming takes a few days to reach your account, and you sell at that day's NAV, which floats — so it is liquid, but it is not a same-second ATM withdrawal and it is not capital-stable while you hold it.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver who needs an instant, never-falling cash buffer, the myth is pointing at the right requirement — and the answer is that the *emergency fund* belongs in a deposit, not that unit trusts are a trap. For the saver leaving long-horizon money in a low-interest account specifically because they believe a fund would "lock it away", it changes the answer: an open-end fund redeems on any business day with no penalty, so accessibility is not the reason to stay out — the only real constraints are a few days' settlement and a floating price, neither of which is a lock.
