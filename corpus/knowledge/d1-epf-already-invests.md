# "EPF already invests for me, why double up"

> Acknowledges EPF's statutory guaranteed-minimum dividend; frames a unit trust / PRS as a top-up for a shortfall, never as safer than or a replacement for EPF. Figures dated; not personalised advice.

## English

### The myth

"My EPF is already invested in the market and pays a solid dividend with a government floor under it. Why would I take my own money and invest it again somewhere riskier — that's just doubling up on the same thing."

### The true part

EPF is an excellent core and deserves the respect the myth gives it. It carries a **statutory guaranteed-minimum 2.5% dividend under the EPF Act 1991 §27** — a floor a unit trust does not have — and it has declared well above that floor in recent years. For a salaried worker, it is a strong, low-effort, partially-guaranteed retirement engine, and nothing here argues for moving money *out* of it or implies a fund is *safer* than it. The instinct to lean on EPF is correct.

### The variable it ignores

The ignored variable is **whether EPF alone closes the gap to the goal — and the two structural ceilings that often mean it doesn't** (corpus/proof/epf-shortfall-cases.md). EPF's contribution is capped to a percentage of salary and its money is locked until retirement, so for many households the projected EPF balance falls short of the income their actual retirement will need — the shortfall cases work through how a comfortable-retirement target outruns the EPF path for a mid-career earner. A top-up is not "doubling up on the same thing"; it is filling the part of the goal EPF's caps and lock can't reach. And the top-up has a lever EPF doesn't: a **PRS contribution earns a separate RM3,000 standalone tax relief**, a different line from the EPF+life RM4,000 sub-cap (corpus/knowledge/b-relief-stacking.md), worth real money back each year (sunset YA2030). The honest framing is a layered one — EPF as the guaranteed-floor core, a fund or PRS as the growth-and-relief top-up for the shortfall — not a contest in which one wins.

### Who it changes the answer for

For a saver whose projected EPF already covers their retirement need and who has filled their reliefs, the myth is reasonable — additional market risk may be optional for them, and EPF doing the job is a fine outcome. For the saver facing a real EPF shortfall (the common case for anyone wanting more than a basic retirement, or self-employed with thin EPF), it changes the answer: a top-up isn't redundant exposure to "the same thing" — it's the only money working on the part of the goal EPF's caps and lock leave uncovered, with a tax relief attached for doing it.
